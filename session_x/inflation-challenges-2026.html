Challenge #3 — energy, geopolitics & trade policy volatility

  • Energy can swing headline inflation quickly (down or up)
  • Geopolitical shocks hit commodities and supply chains first
  • Trade policy and tariffs can reintroduce price pressure (and uncertainty)

Talk track (2 minutes):

  • In the euro area flash estimate for Jan 2026, energy is negative while services is still high: that’s the “headline looks fine, underlying still sticky” pattern.
  • Even if inflation is near target, energy and trade shocks are still tail risks that force central banks to stay cautious.

Sources:

  • Eurostat flash estimate shows energy inflation at -4.1% y/y and services at 3.2% y/y in Jan 2026. https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/web/products-euro-indicators/w/2-04022026-ap
  • IMF WEO Update (Jan 2026) highlights that geopolitical tensions and trade measures can disrupt supply chains and commodity prices. https://www.imf.org/en/publications/weo/issues/2026/01/19/world-economic-outlook-update-january-2026

Challenge #4 — food prices, climate shocks, and “renewed” inflation risk

  • Food inflation is socially and politically high-stakes
  • Climate patterns (e.g., El Niño) can hit harvests and global food prices
  • Even if overall markets are well supplied, shocks can be sharp and localized

Talk track (2 minutes):

  • Treat food and energy as “fast-moving” inflation components: the macro trend can be down while household experience jumps month-to-month.

Sources:

  • ECB Economic Bulletin focus box discusses how El Niño can affect global food commodities and prices. https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/economic-bulletin/focus/2023/html/ecb.ebbox202306_01~36e78cc75e.en.html
  • Guardian (Feb 2026) reports on forecasts for a possible El Niño in 2026. https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2026/feb/08/global-weather-el-nino-pacific-ocean-high-temperatures-2027
  • World Bank food security update summarizes recent commodity market conditions and ongoing input-cost issues. https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/agriculture/brief/food-security-update

Challenge #5 — business stress: margins, refinancing, insolvencies

  • The inflation hangover shows up in cash flow (input costs) and credit (refinancing at higher rates)
  • A slowdown + tight credit conditions tend to hit SMEs first
  • Rising bankruptcies create second-round effects: job losses, supplier defaults, tighter lending

Talk track (2–3 minutes):

  • Emphasize that “inflation problems” aren’t only about CPI prints; they show up in business failures and labor-market scarring.

Source:

  • Allianz Global Insolvency Outlook 2026-27 expects global business insolvencies to rise +6% in 2025 and +5% in 2026, reaching record highs, with higher interest rates straining debt-heavy firms. https://www.allianz.com/en/economic_research/insights/publications/specials_fmo/251021-insolvency-outlook.html

Challenge #6 — fiscal pressure: the “interest bill” problem

  • Governments face tighter trade-offs: debt service vs. investment vs. social support
  • Broad subsidies can reduce pain now but may worsen inflation or debt sustainability
  • Fiscal-monetary mix matters more when inflation is close to target

Talk track (2 minutes):

  • The 2021–2023 inflation episode left higher price levels, and the response left higher interest rates for longer.
  • That makes the cost of debt a constraint, and it feeds back into growth and social cohesion.

Sources:

  • IMF blog (Apr 2025) notes rising debt levels and that high interest rates can limit essential spending on social programs and public investment. https://www.imf.org/en/blogs/articles/2025/04/23/rising-global-debt-requires-countries-to-put-their-fiscal-house-in-order
  • IMF Fiscal Monitor media center notes that interest rates have increased considerably and the interest bill outlook is uncertain. https://mediacenter.imf.org/news/imf—october-2025-fiscal-monitor/s/a770da3b-295d-41ec-b133-41ba455e02b0

Central banks in 2026: disinflation, but no victory lap

  • Fed: holding 3.5%–3.75%, with inflation “somewhat elevated”
  • BoE: Bank Rate 3.75%; sees inflation returning near target, but watches wage/services persistence
  • ECB: deposit facility 2.00% (current operational anchor)

Talk track (2–3 minutes):

  • This is the “tightrope”: cut too early and risk persistence; wait too long and risk unnecessary slowdown + financial stress.
  • Use the IMF chart to highlight that inflation paths differ by economy—so policy is less synchronized than in the early post-pandemic tightening phase.

Sources:

  • Fed FOMC statement (Jan 28, 2026): target range 3-1/2 to 3-3/4 percent; inflation remains somewhat elevated. https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260128a.htm
  • Bank of England Monetary Policy Report (Feb 2026): vote 5–4 to hold at 3.75; discussion of inflation returning around target and services/wage pressures. https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy-report/2026/february-2026
  • ECB official interest rates table shows deposit facility rate at 2.00% (effective 11 Jun 2025). https://www.ecb.europa.eu/stats/policy_and_exchange_rates/key_ecb_interest_rates/html/index.en.html
  • IMF WEO Update (Jan 2026) figure and inflation discussion. https://financing.desa.un.org/sites/default/files/2026-01/IMF%20World%20Economic%20outlook%20update%202026JAN.pdf

Disinflation ≠ relief: the price level is the problem

  • People experience levels (rent, groceries, energy bills), not just rates
  • High-price essentials crowd out discretionary spending
  • Perceived unfairness (“prices imposed”) becomes a political and workplace issue

Talk track (2 minutes):

  • Inflation falling from 8% to 2% does not rewind prices — it slows the rate of further increases.
  • That helps central banks, but households still face a higher baseline for necessities.

Good prompt for discussion: “What costs feel structural for your team/customers?”

Sources:

  • TIME affordability article provides examples of persistent level effects (housing, energy, food) and ongoing salience even as inflation cools. https://time.com/collections/davos-2026/7339214/global-affordability-crisis-cost-of-living-inflation-politics/
  • UNCTAD WESP 2026 emphasizes that high prices continue to erode real incomes and increase inequality even as inflation eases. https://unctad.org/publication/world-economic-situation-and-prospects-2026

Challenge #1 — the “last mile”: services inflation & wages

  • Services inflation is still high in many advanced economies
  • Wage growth and inflation expectations matter more when goods disinflation fades
  • Policy risk: easing too soon can re-ignite persistence

Talk track (2 minutes):

  • Goods prices normalized as supply chains healed; now the remaining pressure is often “domestic” (services + labor costs).
  • Services is also where measurement and lags can be trickier.

Anchors:

  • Eurostat flash: services inflation in the euro area was 3.2% in Jan 2026. https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/web/products-euro-indicators/w/2-04022026-ap
  • ECB speech (Jun 2025): headline around target, but services inflation “still has some distance to travel” for sustainable target stabilization. https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/key/date/2025/html/ecb.sp250624~6bc6bae5ac.en.html
  • Bank of England MPR (Feb 2026): notes services price inflation and wage growth still need to fall further for confidence about sustainable 2%. https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy-report/2026/february-2026

Challenge #2 — housing: the slow-moving inflation engine

  • Housing is both a price-level shock and a monetary-policy transmission channel
  • Supply constraints make housing inflation “sticky”
  • Higher rates can cool demand, but can also suppress new supply (construction) and keep rents elevated

Talk track (2–3 minutes):

  • In many places, housing is the dominant driver of “unaffordability,” even when headline inflation is near target.
  • Housing also links directly to wage bargaining and labor mobility.

Use one concrete statistic to make the point:

  • TIME cites U.S. home prices up about 45% since 2020 as a key driver of affordability stress. https://time.com/collections/davos-2026/7339214/global-affordability-crisis-cost-of-living-inflation-politics/

If you want a UK-focused angle:

  • Bank of England discusses how restrictive policy continues to weigh on interest-rate-sensitive consumption/investment (transmission lags still matter). https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy-report/2026/february-2026

Where inflation stands right now (Jan/Feb 2026 snapshot)

  • Euro area: headline 1.7% y/y (flash, Jan 2026)
  • UK: CPI inflation 3.4% (Dec) — expected back around 2% from April
  • US: Fed funds target range held at 3.5%–3.75% (Jan FOMC)
  • Global: headline inflation expected to decline from 4.1% (2025) → 3.8% (2026)

Practical playbook (what to do with this)

  • Pricing & contracts: shorten price locks, use indexation where acceptable, monitor competitor resets
  • Wages & retention: focus on essentials (housing/transport/childcare), communicate total rewards clearly
  • Procurement & supply: dual-source critical inputs; build “shock clauses” for energy/transport
  • Finance: review debt maturities; stress-test cash flow under “rates stay high” + “growth slows” scenarios
  • Customer & citizen empathy: redesign journeys around affordability (smaller packs, cheaper tiers, flexible plans)

This is the “so what” slide. Invite the room to pick one action per area.

Prompt: “If inflation stays near target but prices remain high, what do we change first?”

Five takeaways to land

  1. Lower inflation doesn’t mean lower prices — affordability can stay politically and economically dominant.
  2. The remaining inflation risk is increasingly domestic: services, wages, housing.
  3. “Shockable” components (energy/food/trade) still drive volatility and policy caution.
  4. Higher rates are now part of the inflation story: refinancing stress, insolvencies, and fiscal constraints.
  5. The winning strategy is resilience: scenario planning + flexibility in pricing, supply, and balance sheets.
