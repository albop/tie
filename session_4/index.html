The PSZ / Auten-Splinter Debate

PSZ Debate

In Income Inequality in the United States: Using Tax Data to Measure Long-Term Trends (JPE, 2024), Auten and Splinter challenge Piketty, Saez and Zucman‚Äôs estimates of US income inequality trends.

What are they measuring?

  • Shared goal: estimate the distribution of (broad) U.S. national income, not just ‚Äútaxable income‚Äù
  • PSZ (DINA approach): combine tax + survey + national accounts so the distribution sums to 100% of national income
    • Produces series of pre-tax and post-tax income shares consistent with macro totals[^1]
  • Auten‚ÄìSplinter (AS): start from tax data but adjust for
    • tax-base and reporting changes over time
    • demographic/social changes (tax units vs persons, filing behavior)
    • ‚Äúmissing‚Äù income not on returns
    • Conclude top shares are lower and rise less
  • Core point: both agree tax returns are incomplete ‚Üí disagreement is about how to correct and allocate what‚Äôs missing

Framing: not ‚Äúwho can do arithmetic,‚Äù but ‚Äúwhat is the target concept + what assumptions map observed data to that concept?‚Äù

Disagreement: allocating ‚Äúmissing‚Äù income

Big moving parts (each changes top shares materially):

  • Untaxed / under-taxed business & capital income
    • e.g., how to allocate partnership/pass-through income that is large and unevenly reported
  • Corporate profits / retained earnings & incidence
    • who ‚Äúreceives‚Äù retained earnings (shareholders vs pensions/nonprofits/foreigners) and how to assign corporate taxes
  • Unit & splitting rules
    • tax unit vs individual adults; splitting within couples; treatment of non-filers
  • Transfers and noncash items (for post-tax/post-transfer inequality)
    • valuation and assignment of in-kind benefits; timing of pandemic-era transfers
  • Evasion / underreporting assumptions
    • whether (and how strongly) underreporting is concentrated at the top

Disagreement: allocating ‚Äúmissing‚Äù income

What each side claims:

  • PSZ: AS mis-allocates large amounts of untaxed income away from the top ‚Üí mechanically lowers the top 1% share
  • AS: PSZ reallocations rely on problematic back-of-envelope adjustments and inappropriate distributions ‚Üí AS allocations are more defensible

Takeaway: series are model-based reconstructions:

  • crucial: what is ‚Äúincome‚Äù? what‚Äôs missing? how is it allocated? how sensitive are conclusions to alternatives?

Political Philosophy (1)

Utilitarianism: the state should maximize total utility of all citizens(Bentham, Stuart Mill)

  • marginal utility implies the need for some distribution (because rich citizens derive less utility from an additional consumption unit than the poors)
  • it does not imply perfect redistribution because
    • there is a cost to redistribution
    • too much equality can induce bad incentives which is detrimental to everybody
  • Modern version: Pareto weights \(\omega_i\) \[\max \sum_i \omega_i U(c_i)\]

Political Philosophy (2)

Liberalism: the state should choose fair policies as evaluated by an objective observer hidden behind a veil of ignorance

  • John Rawls proposes a mental experiment where one evaluates the rules of the society (including the economic system) from an original position untainted by the ultimate place of the observer in the society
  • reducing inequalities could be seen as insurance against a bad draw
  • but what is the risk? when probabilities are unknown one talks about ambiguity, or radical uncertainty
  • it is not clear what is the aversion to this kind of risk
    • Rawls/Knight: one maximizes utility in the worst case (maximin criterion)
  • more redistribution than in the utilitarian case
    • but not 100% equal either

Political Philosophy (3)

Libertarianism: state should punish crimes, and help secure voluntary contracts between willing individuals, but it should not redistribute income

  • inequality is not relevant at a state‚Äôs level
    • in the same way that granting the same grade to all students is not a valid objective
  • it doesn‚Äôt mean libertarians are not altruistic
  • state must guarantee that the allocation procedure be legal
    • concretely: grant property rights

Inequality and Development

Kuznets has studied the relationship between the level of development and inequality.

He asserted that countries develop in two phases:

  • in developing economies: entrepreneurs accumulate wealth by hiring cheap workers migrating from the country-side to the cities
  • in mature economies: salaries rise and growth in human capital becomes central to economic growth

Inequality and Growth (1)

What if there was a trade-off between growth and inequality:

  • trickle-down economics:
    • transferring money to the rich benefits everyone because it is reinvested in the economy
    • popular with some conservative politicians
    • ‚Ä¶but not defended by any serious economist
  • some growth economists (Paul Romer, Philippe Aghion) have studied how growth induces more inequality
    • through skill-biased technical changes
    • through the unequal distribution of the rents from innovation

Inequality and Growth (2)

Empirically, several studies have been carried out see whether there was a systematic relationship between growth of inequalities.
